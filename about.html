About this small, happy website

We built an entire site around thirteen characters of output. Here's why that isn't as silly as it sounds. (Okay — it's a little silly. On purpose.)

01 · Purpose

Why a whole site for two words?

Hello World is a homage. Not to a product, a company, or a framework — but to a moment. The moment a person types their first program, presses run, and the machine answers back. Nothing about software gets more honest than that.

Since Brian Kernighan first jotted "hello, world" into a Bell Labs tutorial in 1972, those two words have opened nearly every programming book, tutorial, and README ever written. They are the closest thing our craft has to a folk song — passed down, re-sung in every new language, always the same melody.

This site exists to celebrate that. We collect the examples, tell the history, and keep a small flame lit for the feeling every developer remembers: the first time the computer said hello back.

No sign-ups. No newsletter pop-ups. No roadmap. Just a warm welcome to the craft, printed to standard output.

mission.c
/* our entire mission,
   as a program */
#include <stdio.h>

int main(void) {
    while (people_keep_learning()) {
        printf("Hello, World!\n");
        printf("Welcome. You belong here.\n");
    }
    return 0; /* never reached */
}
02 · Principles

What we believe

A site this small can still have convictions. Ours fit in four cards — roughly one per line of a hello world program.

>_

Small is beautiful

The best first program is the shortest one that works. We apply the same rule to this site: no bloat, no build step, no dependencies you can't read in an afternoon.

::

Beginnings deserve ceremony

Every expert was once a beginner squinting at a terminal. We think that moment deserves a monument — even a tiny, slightly goofy one like this.

\n

Joy is a feature

Programming can be stressful, abstract, and humbling. Hello world is none of those things. It's pure delight, and we protect that on every page.

*

Everyone gets a hello

The greeting is universal by design. Whatever language you speak — human or programming — this tradition already includes you.

03 · README.md

Frequently asked, honestly answered

Every good project ships with a README. Here's ours.

who-runs-this
A very small crew of programmers who never quite got over their first successful compile. We maintain the site in our spare time, usually late at night, usually with tea. You can reach us at hello@hello-world-9677.sites.tibly.ai or, for the truly old-fashioned, at 42 Kernighan Lane, Suite 0, Murray Hill, NJ 07974 · (555) 019-7201.
is-this-a-product
No. There is nothing to buy, subscribe to, or install. The whole site is the product, and it's free the way a wave from a stranger is free.
can-i-contribute
Yes — send us your first hello world story, or the program in a language we haven't featured yet. The stranger the language, the better. Brainfuck submissions are read with equal parts respect and concern.
why-the-comma
"Hello, World!" with the comma is the K&R canonical form, and we are traditionalists. That said, we won't return a non-zero exit code if you leave it out. We're joyful, not pedantic.
exit-code
Always 0. That's a promise.
